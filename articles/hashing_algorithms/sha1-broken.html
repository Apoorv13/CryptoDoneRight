Did you see the news or a news post which states “SHA1 is broken. Beware!” or “Google just broke SHA1.” Or “At death's door for years, widely used SHA1 function is now dead” or “Stop using SHA1: It's now completely unsafe!”? or Did a colleague, friend or an acquaintance mention about SHA1?

Understanding Collisions

A collision or clash occurs when two different inputs to a function, typically one used to compress large data items into a smaller or fixed size, produce the same output, called (depending on the application) a hash value, checksum, fingerprint, or digest.
Note: Such attacks are very rare due to its difficulty

Recently, SHA-1 was “SHAttered” or broken and an attack was developed wherein it is now practically possible to craft two colliding PDF files and obtain a SHA-1 digital signature on the first PDF file which can also be abused as a valid signature on the second PDF file. Even though SHA-1 is broken, Do NOT Panic.

A pictorial representation of what a collision looks like:

If you watch here closely, there are two PDF files. Notice that one document is BLUE and the other RED. This makes this different document, meaning their SHA1 hashes are supposed to be different, right? Unfortunately, Google was able to use some extreme math and computing power to be able to have these two PDF files have the same SHA1 hashes.

What does this entail? Here is an example that would help you understand this in a simple way:
By crafting the two colliding PDF files as two rental agreements with different rent, it is possible to trick someone to create a valid signature for a high-rent contract by having him or her sign a low-rent contract. If SHA1 is hash values are the same, cryptographically, this means that these two documents are the same which is NOT good. We will let you ponder on some more implications of these.

How can the above description be translated into an attack? Imagine that second file is malware or a forged document, like a certificate designed to guarantee the authenticity of a website, such as Google.com. An attacker could have their malware or their fake website trusted by any system that checks SHA-1 hashes to verify. "Another use case could be if you are using application whitelisting on your computer, which uses hashes to verify that the files are what they claim to be, it could be possible for a malware file to have the same hash as for example Word or some other trusted application.

Now, please do NOT panic.

Most major browsers are going to slowly stop trusting certificates that are signed by SHA1. Google Chrome has announced that they are planning to stop support for SHA1 by the end of January 2017: Read More

Similarly, Microsoft has also announced plans to deprecate SHA-1 by the middle of the year. When that happens, it'll follow in Google's footsteps so that its Internet Explorer and Edge browsers will prevent sites protected with a SHA-1 certificate from loading and will display a warning. Here is a quick pictorial representation of this:

The Browser will show a green lock sign on the address bar if a certificate is trusted and good.

Even Firefox is planning to do the same this year too. Interestingly Github is also have some issues with SHA1, please click here for more info.

Here is some more information that will help you understand the severity or the depth of this news: The SHAttered attack required over 9,223,372,036,854,775,808 SHA1 computations. This took the equivalent processing power as 6,500 years of single-CPU computations and 110 years of single-GPU computations.

The attackers must be an extremely well-funded. The attack is not within the reach of anyone, but is within the reach of major organizations and nation-states. Given the computational expense of carrying out such a collision, it is obvious that executing such an attack is sensible for only someone who has vested a great amount of interest and has the computational powers (monetary) required.

But, the SHAttered attack is 100,000 faster than the brute force attack that relies on the birthday paradox (Wiki Link). The brute force attack would require 12,000,000 GPU years to complete, and it is therefore impractical.
